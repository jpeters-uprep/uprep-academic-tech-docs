## Organization and Prioritization

Folders are crucial for students being able to access the relevant and current
resources in your course. Please identify your folders with a meaningful title
and heading. Other options include color coding certain folders. Try and
envision how students will navigate the structure of your course to find what
they need. A good rule of thumb is that the first page of the course should
contain folders, under which all of your course content resides. Common
strategies include:

- Folders for each unit,
- Folders for certain date ranges, or
- Folders for types of material (e.g., Homework, Readings, Daily Practice)

If there is a resource, link, or activity that students will need to access
regularly throughout the course, consider putting it in a separate folder (such
as “Toolkit” or “Common Resources”) at the top of your page.

Keep materials no more than three levels deep: students should never need more
than two clicks from your course home page to reach a resource — your course
(level 1) → a unit or topic folder (level 2) → at most one more folder of
related materials (level 3). If you find yourself nesting folders inside folders
inside folders, it's a sign to flatten your structure.

Be careful with "Unpublish." Unpublishing a folder doesn't just hide it from
students — depending on your school's Schoology configuration, it can also pull
every assignment inside that folder out of the gradebook. Don't use Unpublish as
a way to tidy up a cluttered course; archive old folders instead, or ask June if
you're not sure which to use.

## Due Dates

![Due Dates screenshot](/img/schoology-walkthrough/dueDates.png)

The class calendar is the most common time management and organizational tool
that students use to organize their tasks, so complete and accurate due dates
make a real difference for student organization. See
[Schoology Requirements](/faculty/schoology/requirements) for which assignments
must be calendared.

## Submission Method

![Submission screenshot](/img/schoology-walkthrough/submission.png)

By default, Schoology assumes that assignments will be submitted electronically.
If you do not want to receive this assignment through Schoology, you must turn
off the "Submissions" feature while creating the assignment. Otherwise,
Schoology will assume that the assignment is missing, and erroneously notify
students that their work is missing. This has the unfortunate side effect of
training students to ignore all missing assignment notifications, whether
accurate or not.

## Resources

![Resources screenshot](/img/schoology-walkthrough/resources.png)

Schoology allows you to attach links or supplemental files to an assignment. If
there are files or websites which a student should use to complete an
assignment, please attach them directly to the assignment so that students may
easily find them. If the resource will be used beyond that assignment, for
example throughout a unit or throughout the term, consider putting it in the
most appropriate folder and leaving instructions on where students will find it.

## Workload Calendar

![Workload Calendar screenshot](/img/schoology-walkthrough/workloadCal.png)

The Workload Calendar is a separate class for each grade level, 9-12, which
exists to show us the overall picture of how many assignments, assessments,
etc., students are balancing at a time. Once you have created an assignment,
copy it to the appropriate grade level Workload Calendar using the "Copy to
Course" feature.

See the
[Upper School Workload Calendaring Expectations](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1LXi36Y2kmOI2TS5mjosCYKOsxOOtYzpQRuZYGQtYOvA/edit?usp=sharing)

## Grading

UPrep's preferred gradebook tool is the one integrated in Schoology, which
offers several attractive benefits for teachers and students:

- The gradebook is automatically populated when you create your assignments,
  meaning you do not have to maintain a separate gradebook.
- Your feedback to students will be linked to the assignment directly so it is
  easy for them to see your comments or score.
- As many classes already use the gradebook, students will likely be familiar
  with how to navigate it and see your comments.
- Students and parents frequently cite the gradebook as one of the most helpful
  tools in supporting students' performance in class, and in particular tracking
  overdue or missing work.

![Grading screenshot](/img/schoology-walkthrough/grading-1.jpg)

Please consider these guidelines:

- Publish grades to students either:
  - In your class if useful for discussion or feedback sessions, or
  - Outside of school hours, so as not to pull students' attention from their
    other activities.
- If you want to weight certain assignments by either term, or category, do this
  at the beginning of the course as much as possible. Changing weighting during
  the term may change a student's grade in a way which is surprising to them.
- Familiarize yourself with the options to show or hide overall grades in the
  Grade Setup screen according to your preferences. In Middle School, please
  disable all overall grades (a percentage output) so as not to confuse students
  with an "overall percentage grade" which is in contrast to our Standards-Based
  Grading.
- Take a few minutes in your class to orient students to where you will post
  feedback. You may wish to leave specific, detailed feedback on the assignment
  submission, for example, or high-level summary comments in the gradebook. You
  may use either, both, or neither of those options, but please be consistent
  and communicate your intent to students.
- Familiarize yourself with Gradebook Exceptions (flags), as they are convenient
  tools to communicate necessary information to students — see below.

See [Schoology Requirements](/faculty/schoology/requirements) for gradebook
backup and accuracy requirements that apply regardless of how you grade.

### Gradebook Exceptions (Flags)

Hover over any gradebook cell and click the flag icon to apply an exception.
Exceptions let you communicate something about a student's submission status
without changing the raw score. Schoology currently offers six exception types:

- **Missing** — functions the same as a zero in grade calculations. Use this
  only when you don't want the student to be able to submit the work anymore.
- **Incomplete** — stays on the student's Overdue list and does not affect the
  grade calculation. Use this if you're accepting a late submission but don't
  want the gap to count against the student in the meantime.
- **Exempt** — removes the item from grade calculations entirely, so it neither
  helps nor hurts the student's grade. (Called "Excused" in older Schoology
  versions.)
- **Absent, Late, and Collected** — additional exceptions that remove the item
  from a student's Overdue notifications; use whichever best describes the
  submission's status.

See Schoology's
[Use Exceptions in the Gradebook documentation](https://uc.powerschool-docs.com/en/schoology/latest/use-exceptions-in-the-gradebook)
for full definitions.

### Copying Grade Settings to Another Course

If you teach multiple sections or courses with the same grading structure, you
don't need to rebuild Grade Setup from scratch each time. From Grade Setup,
click Copy Settings in the upper right, choose which elements to copy
(Categories, Grading Scales, and/or Rubrics), and select the destination
course(s). You must be a Course Admin on the destination course to copy settings
into it.

A word of caution: Copy Settings does not copy your Visibility Settings, Grading
Periods, or Final Grade Settings. You'll still need to configure those
individually for each course, according to your division's requirements (see
above).

See Schoology's
[Grade Setup documentation](https://uc.powerschool-docs.com/en/schoology/latest/courses-grade-setup)
for more detail.
